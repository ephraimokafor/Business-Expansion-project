INTRODUCTION
 The CEO and CMO want a concise, trustworthy view of how the online retail store performed historically and which areas offer the greatest growth opportunities. The main deliverables requested were four visuals answering: Monthly revenue time series for 2011 (seasonality/forecasting). Top 10 revenue-generating countries (excluding the UK) showing both revenue and quantity sold. Top 10 customers by revenue, sorted descending (customer ranking). Demand by country/region (excluding the UK) on a single view for expansion decisions. The senior team needs correct visuals because management decisions (expansion, marketing spend, stock planning, VIP customer programs) will be made from these charts.

/> />

Problem statement: Senior management faces three practical problems: Forecasting & capacity planning: Without clear month-by-month seasonality, the CEO can’t forecast inventory needs or marketing timing. Geographic expansion decisions: The CEO needs a global view of demand (excluding the UK) to prioritise countries/regions for expansion. Customer retention & revenue concentration: The CMO needs to know which customers generate most revenue (top 10) to protect and grow that segment while ensuring concentration risk is understood. Underlying these problems is a data quality risk: transactional records include negative quantities (returns) and erroneous unit prices, which must be removed/handled before analysis.
  • Objectives : Primary aims: Produce accurate, easy-to-interpret visuals answering the four questions above. Clean the transactional data to ensure all analysis is trustworthy (filter returns/errors). Deliver actionable, prioritised recommendations for growth, marketing, product stocking, and customer retention. Success criteria: Each visual clearly answers the business question with no misleading axes, correct sorting, and appropriate granularity. Forecast inputs incorporate observed seasonality (monthly) for 2011 and support a short-term forecast. The geographic map shows all countries (excluding the UK) in a single, scroll-free view, highlighting target expansion opportunities. Data & Data cleaning (methodology – stage 1) Files seen: visuals in the folder (images), but not the raw dataset. Below are the clean-up steps to run on the transactional dataset prior to analysis: Load the transactional data (fields expected: InvoiceNo, StockCode, Description, Quantity, InvoiceDate, UnitPrice, CustomerID, Country). Type conversion & timestamping: parse InvoiceDate to datetime; create Year, Month, MonthYear fields. Remove cancelled transactions/returns: filter out rows where Quantity < 1. (If returns are recorded as negative quantities but need to be included as return analytics, then isolate them to a separate returns analysis — they must not be mixed into positive sales aggregates.) Remove invalid unit prices: filter out rows where UnitPrice <= 0. (Alternatively, if there are a few zeroes that look like legitimate promotions, validate manually.) Handle missing CustomerIDs: if CustomerID is missing, decide — either exclude from customer ranking or attempt imputation only if safe. Deduplicate/remove test data: remove test invoices, cancelled rows or clearly erroneous stock codes. Create derived metrics: Revenue = Quantity * UnitPrice; aggregate at month/country/customer levels as needed. Validation checks: totals before and after cleaning; random row inspection; check for anomalies (very large unit price or quantity outliers). (These checks implement the exact conditional formulas you were asked to apply: Quantity >= 1 and UnitPrice > 0.) Analysis methodology (stage 2) High-level steps to produce the visuals and insight: Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): Produce summary stats (total revenue, total qty, orders, customers, average order value). Visualise distributions (unit price, revenue per invoice, qty per invoice). Time series aggregation (Question 1): Filter data for Year = 2011. Aggregate Revenue by Month (Jan–Dec). Visualise monthly revenue as a line chart with markers; overlay 12-month moving average and annotate peaks/troughs and notable events (promos/holidays). Fit a forecasting model (see Modelling below) for a short-term forecast. Top countries (Question 2): Exclude UK (Country != 'United Kingdom'). Aggregate by country: Revenue and Quantity. Sort by Revenue descending and select the top 10. Visual: dual-axis or combined grouped bar: revenue as bars (descending left to right), quantity as line or second bars. Add data labels and currency formatting. Top customers (Question 3): Aggregate Revenue by CustomerID. Sort descending and take the top 10 customers. Visual: horizontal bar chart sorted descending (largest at top). Use consistent colour highlighting top 3. Include CustomerID + revenue, and optionally number of orders or average order value, to profile them. Demand by country/region (Question 4): Exclude the UK. Aggregate Quantity or Revenue by country. Visual: world choropleth or proportional symbol map (circle size ~ demand). Because the CEO asked for a single view, no hovering or scrolling, ensure the map fits one screen with clear labels — or provide a sorted table plus choropleth side-by-side. Use a diverging/stepped colour palette, but since we only deliver images, ensure the legend is clear. KPIs & Dashboard: Create a one-page dashboard with: Total Revenue (YTD), Revenue growth vs prior year, Average Order Value, Total Customers, Repeat Rate, Top Country, Top Customer, and Returns %. Validation & story checks: Confirm monotonic sorting, axis scales, dual-axis pitfalls avoided (prefer shared scale or clear labelling), and consistent currency units.
    • Modelling (what I recommend and why) You asked for forecasting and identifying growth opportunities — below are the modelling choices and the intended outputs. A. Time series forecasting (for the monthly 2011 → next year forecast) Models to try: Facebook/Meta Prophet (quick, handles seasonality & holidays), SARIMA (for interpretability), and an ensemble if desired. Input: Monthly revenue aggregated for 2011 (plus earlier years if available). Output: 12-month forecast with 80% & 95% confidence intervals, and decomposition into trend/seasonality/residuals. Evaluation: backtest using rolling origin (if earlier years present) and metrics MAE, RMSE. B. Customer segmentation & CLV modelling Technique: RFM analysis (Recency, Frequency, Monetary) to segment customers; K-means or hierarchical clustering to formalise segments. Advanced: Predictive CLV model (e.g., BG/NBD + Gamma-Gamma or machine learning regression model) to rank customers by expected future value. Use: identify VIPs, retention priority, and target promotions. C. Geospatial demand & expansion scoring Technique: Rank countries by a multi-factor score: recent revenue growth rate, revenue per capita (or per online user proxy), quantity sold, average order value, returns rate, and logistics cost proxy. Output: priority list of countries with expected ROI for expansion. D. Product performance (if product data available) Technique: ABC analysis (Pareto) to identify top stock-keeping units by revenue; support product bundling and stocking decisions. E. NLP / CV (optional advanced) NLP: Analyse customer reviews/feedback for sentiment and product pain points. CV: If product images & returns have visual reasons (damaged goods), computer vision can help categorise.

Visualisation choices — recommended chart types (mapping to your questions)
I aligned the visuals to what leadership asked for and to best practices: Q1 (2011 monthly revenue): Line chart with monthly points (Jan–Dec), 12-month moving average overlay and seasonal decomposition panel. Annotate major peaks (holiday season, promotion weeks) for context. (You already have 2011 MONTHLY SALES TREND.png in the folder — that’s the right visual form.) Q2 (Top 10 countries by revenue + quantity, exclude UK): Grouped bar chart or bar + line (combo): Bars = Revenue (descending), Line = Quantity or second axis for quantity. Add data labels and exclude the UK filter. Alternative: small multiples showing revenue and quantity side-by-side for each country. Q3 (Top 10 customers by revenue): Horizontal sorted bar chart (largest to smallest) with customer IDs on y-axis, revenue on x-axis. Highlight the top 3 customers with a different colour and show the secondary metric (orders or average order value) as a label. Q4 (Demand by country excluding UK): Choropleth world map with countries shaded by demand or a bubble map (circle size = demand). Since the CEO requested one single view with no hovering/scrolling, also include a small ranked table (top 15) next to the map so numbers are visible immediately. Dashboard / KPIs: One-page with KPI tiles, a sparkline for trend, the monthly line chart, top countries bar, and top customer bar — made in Power BI or Tableau. Use a consistent colour palette and clear currency formatting.

Data analytics — what to inspect in each visual (how management should read them) Q1 monthly trend: Look for recurring peaks (holiday season, end of quarter) and troughs. Compute month-over-month and year-over-year % changes. If one or two months concentrate a high share of revenue, plan promotions and inventory accordingly. Q2 top countries: Compare revenue share vs population/marketing spend to detect efficiency. High revenue + low quantity → high AOV markets (premium buyers). High quantity + low revenue → high volume low price markets (operate differently for logistics & pricing). Q3 top customers: Identify the share of revenue contributed by the top 10 (Pareto check). If a small group contributes >40–50%, that’s concentration risk. For top customers, compute churn risk (recency), repeat purchase rate, and average order value. Q4 demand by country/region: Rank countries by absolute demand and growth rate. Also, evaluate returns % by country (high returns could indicate product mismatch or delivery issues).
